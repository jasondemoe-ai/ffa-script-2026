Call to Convention — Officer Speeches
Alex — Center Stage
Over 100 years ago in 1925, the Future Farmers of Virginia was founded. Three years later in 1928, the Future Farmers of America had their first national convention, held in Kansas City, Missouri. In 1929 the great state of Oregon was chartered in this organization! The year after that we got our official colors and creed established, and then in 1933 we decided on this blue jacket which would turn into a symbol of history and respect. What started as some farm boys in Virginia with a want to further their Agriculture knowledge has transformed into an organization with nearly 16,000 members in Oregon and over a million across the country. We are an organization that promotes premier leadership, personal growth, and career success, but we could not do that without the past to lead us to this point. E.M. Tiffany phrased it best when he said, “…even as the better things we now enjoy have come to us from the struggles of former years.” These former years are the roots of your organization, without them we wouldn't get to grow to where we are today. They have shaped us, taught us, and are preparing us for the future. I ask you this week, Oregon FFA, to think about your roots, what are your roots and how have they brought you here?
Henry — Center Stage
Legacy carries a weight that's hard to put into words, yet it's woven into every corner of agriculture and FFA. It lives in the farmers who rise before the sun to tend to their animals and their land, in the long days spent planting and harvesting, and in the quiet, exhausting hours in the farrowing barns when challenges seem endless. Still, farmers press on—not because it's easy, but because they feel a deep responsibility to their animals, their families, and themselves. Through grit, resilience, and unwavering dedication, they shape a legacy built from everyday choices and hard-earned victories, one that will echo long after their work is done. And when we talk about forging a path for future generations, we're really talking about carrying that legacy forward—honoring the past while building something even stronger for those who will follow.
Kendall — Center Stage
Agriculture is community. Agriculture is the basis of our world. It creates bonds, connection, and a place where we can grow together. After a day at school and a pretty fun chapter activity at Helix, my friend Evan invited two of us to visit his farm late at night to see their equipment and farming operation. Filled with excitement, we headed over to their place. The wealth of knowledge he shared made an impact. Evan, with his willingness to share and his love for agriculture, welcomes people in, ready to create bonds that will last forever. Agriculture is more than what we see on the surface. It is early mornings, late nights, and the patience to wait for growth you cannot always see right away. It is trusting the process, caring for the land, and knowing that what you plant today will shape what comes tomorrow. Agriculture is family.
Lydia — Center Stage
And on the eighth day, God looked down on his planned paradise and said, “I need a caretaker.” So God made a farmer. God said, “I need somebody willing to get up before dawn, milk cows, work all day in the field, milk cows again, eat supper, then go to town and stay past midnight at a meeting of the school board.” So God made a farmer. Paul Harvey first delivered those words in 1978, honoring the grit and soul of American agriculture. He understood that a farmer isn't just someone who works the land, they're someone rooted in purpose. Because purpose is what gets you up before dawn. Purpose is what keeps you steady through long days and tough seasons. Purpose is what turns ordinary work into a calling. Inspired by the farmers Harvey described, we as FFA members are called to live with that same unwavering purpose, remaining rooted and committed to something greater than ourselves.
Ethian — Center Stage
Character isn't something we turn on when it's convenient. It's what guides us when the pressure to fit in is strongest. In a world that constantly tells us who to be, our character keeps us grounded in who we are. Abraham Lincoln understood this. Before he was remembered as a great leader, he was known for losing—elections, public support, and opportunities. He was told that if he softened his beliefs, or if he said what people wanted to hear, success would come easier. But Lincoln refused. During the Lincoln/Douglas debates, he chose honesty over popularity, even when it cost him the Senate seat. He wasn't rooted in winning, he was rooted in principle. And years later, that same character made him the leader our nation needed. Those moments reveal what we are truly rooted in. The Lincoln we remember wasn't perfect. He was rooted. Rooted in who he truly was. Rooted in character.
Livvy — Center Stage
In 2024, a 17-year-old FFA member from Eastern Oregon, about to begin her junior year at Imbler High School, faced a moment that would forever redefine her life. Ellie Turley was diagnosed with Hodgkin's Lymphoma at just 17 years old. Starting what would be the hardest fight she's ever faced. Even when everything felt uncertain, there was never a time where Ellie gave up, instead she leaned into what had always been there. She grew her roots deeper, into her family, her friends, her faith, her community, and the strength she had been building long before this diagnosis. Her roots are what held her steady through this fight, showing that the things we are growing now in our everyday lives are the very things that carry us through our toughest moments. Ellie's story reminds us that growing your roots doesn't stop in a single moment. The roots we grow here don't stay in these walls. They show up in our homes, our communities, and the moments when we need strength the most. Ellie didn't just fight, she grew her roots.

What I did not say was that my world was shifting in ways I did not yet have words for. By third grade, the shift felt heavier. I stood in the hallway during a routine health screening. I stepped onto the scale in front of my teacher. She looked at the number and said, “You need a burger and fries… and maybe a milkshake.” I do not know when insecurity first met me, but I know it has stayed with me ever since. What hurt most was not the comment. It was the realization that no one knew what I was carrying.

My days were supposed to have returned to normal years ago. Instead, this became my normal. I was in a new school, surrounded by people I didn't know, trying to find a new “normal” I never asked for. Years of questioning followed: Am I loved? Am I valued? Who am I? And yet, through it all, there was one thing no one could take: my story. The joy of being an Elkton Elk had been stripped from me. I was at a new school, surrounded by faces I barely knew. The hallways were unfamiliar. The classrooms felt different. And I felt different. Insecurity followed me everywhere I went. And here is the part I do not always say out loud. Even now, I still struggle. I still compare myself to others. I still wonder if I am “too quiet.” If I am doing enough. If I am enough. Sometimes I feel like I am still climbing onto that school bus for the first time, still trying to find where I belong. That little girl is not gone. She just grew up. It was not until earlier this year that I began to see my story differently.
